Final Expense Sales Training — Session 12: Opening, Objections, and Compliance

Opening the Call — First Impressions Matter
When you're making that first impression, never say "I'm calling to sell you life insurance."
Instead say "Hi, this is Dana calling on behalf of Senior Life Services. I'm reaching out because you recently requested some information about a program that helps cover final expenses."
The good response is warm, personal, and references their request. The bad response sounds salesy and puts people on the defensive immediately.

Objection — "I already have insurance"
When the prospect says "I already have insurance," don't say "Well, you probably need more."
A better response is: "That's great that you've planned ahead! Can I ask — is your current policy specifically designed for final expenses, or is it more of a general life insurance plan?"
This reframes the conversation without dismissing what they already have. Handle the objection with curiosity, not pressure.

Objection — "I can't afford it"
If they say "I can't afford it," never say "It's really cheap, trust me."
Instead say: "I completely understand — budget is important. Most of our plans work out to about a dollar a day. Would it help if I showed you the most affordable option first?"
Always frame affordability in daily terms, not monthly. This makes the cost feel manageable.

Objection — "I need to think about it"
When they say they need to think about it, don't say "What's there to think about?" That sounds pushy.
A good response is: "Absolutely, take your time. Can I ask what specific questions you'd like to think through? I might be able to help answer them right now."
This keeps the conversation going without applying pressure.

Compliance — Never Make Guarantees
From a compliance standpoint, never guarantee approval. Don't say "You're definitely going to be approved."
The compliant response is: "Based on what you've shared, it looks like you may qualify — but final approval depends on the application review."
Misrepresenting approval certainty is a serious compliance risk and can result in disciplinary action. Always use qualifying language like "may," "could," or "based on the information provided."

Compliance — Recording Disclosure
You must always disclose if the call is being recorded. Never say "Don't worry, nobody's listening."
The correct disclosure is: "I want to let you know that this call may be recorded for quality and training purposes."
Failure to provide proper recording disclosure is a privacy violation and regulatory requirement. This must happen at the opening of every call.

Qualifying Stage — Health Questions
During qualifying, never say "Are you healthy?" — it's too vague and sounds clinical.
Instead, try saying: "I just have a few simple health questions. These help us find the right plan for you. Have you been hospitalized in the last two years?"
Keep health questions conversational and explain why you're asking. This builds trust and reduces discomfort.

Closing — Asking for the Sale
When closing, avoid saying "So, do you want to buy this?" It sounds abrupt.
A good response is: "Based on everything we've discussed, the Premier Plan at $32 a month looks like a great fit. Should I go ahead and get your application started?"
Always summarize the benefits before asking for commitment. The close should feel like a natural next step.
